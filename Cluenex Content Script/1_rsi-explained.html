What is RSI: How Traders Use Momentum to Time Entries and Exits

Definition

RSI (Relative Strength Index) is a momentum oscillator that measures the speed and magnitude of price changes on a scale of 0 to 100, used to identify overbought and oversold conditions in a stock.

Developed by J. Welles Wilder in 1978, RSI quantifies buying and selling pressure by comparing the magnitude of recent gains to recent losses. The indicator oscillates between 0 and 100, with readings above 70 conventionally interpreted as overbought and readings below 30 as oversold. RSI helps traders identify potential reversal points and confirm momentum shifts without requiring price to form obvious chart patterns.

How RSI Works

RSI calculates the ratio of average gains to average losses over a specified period, typically 14 periods (days on daily charts, hours on hourly charts, minutes on minute charts). The formula is:

RSI = 100 - [100 / (1 + RS)]
where RS = Average Gain over 14 periods / Average Loss over 14 periods

The 14-period default balances sensitivity and reliability. Shorter periods (7 or 9) make RSI more responsive to recent price action, producing more signals but also more false signals. Longer periods (21 or 28) smooth the indicator, reducing noise but delaying signal generation.

RSI measures only the magnitude of price changes, not direction. When a stock rallies strongly, buyers are dominating, and RSI rises. When sellers take control and the stock falls, RSI declines. The oscillator is mean-reverting: extremely high RSI readings (above 80) and extremely low readings (below 20) rarely persist, creating opportunities for contrarian trades.

How to Use RSI in Practice

Entry and Exit Signals

RSI generates trading signals through threshold crossovers and divergences. Here are the primary entry and exit scenarios:

Signal Type RSI Trigger Confirmation Action
Oversold Bounce (Buy) RSI drops below 30 RSI crosses back above 30 Enter long on close above 30 or next candle open
Overbought Pullback (Sell) RSI rises above 70 RSI crosses back below 70 Enter short on close below 70
Exit Long RSI exceeds 80 Extreme overbought confirmed Exit half position or set trailing stop
Exit Short RSI falls below 20 Extreme oversold confirmed Cover half position or set buy stop
Bullish Divergence Price new low; RSI doesn't go lower Price bounces above swing high Enter long; selling pressure weakening
Bearish Divergence Price new high; RSI doesn't go higher Price breaks below swing low Enter short; buying pressure fading

Why Divergence Is the High-Probability Signal

Divergence is the most reliable RSI signal because it reveals a shift in momentum before price action becomes obvious.

Bullish Divergence (buy signal): Price makes a new low, but RSI does not go below its previous low. This shows that selling pressure is weakening even as price falls. Enter long when price bounces and closes above the swing high between the two lows.

Bearish Divergence (sell signal): Price makes a new high, but RSI does not exceed its previous high. This shows that buying pressure is fading despite the price reaching new highs. Enter short when price breaks below the swing low between the two highs.

Common Mistakes and Misconceptions

❌ "RSI above 70 means I should short immediately."
Many traders misinterpret overbought as a direct sell signal. In reality, stocks in strong uptrends often remain above 70 for extended periods. RSI above 70 is a warning to tighten stops and take partial profits, not an automatic reversal signal. Wait for RSI to fail to make a new high alongside a new price high (bearish divergence) or for a close below the 70 line before shorting.
❌ "RSI below 30 is always a buy."
Stocks in steep downtrends often stay below 30 for weeks. Blindly buying at RSI 30 catches falling knives. Instead, use RSI 30 as a signal to raise your hand—wait for bullish divergence or a confirmed close above 30 before entering long.
❌ "The 14-period default works on all timeframes."
Many traders apply a 14-period RSI to 1-minute charts and expect the same reliability as on daily charts. Shorter timeframes require shorter RSI periods (5–7) to capture meaningful signals. Longer timeframes (weekly, monthly) can use longer periods (21–28) for fewer, higher-probability setups.
❌ "RSI divergence always works."
While divergence is high-probability, it can fail if the underlying trend remains too strong. A bearish divergence in a bull market may reverse immediately. Always confirm divergence with price action (a close below support) before trading it.
❌ "I should use RSI in isolation."
RSI in isolation leads to whipsaws. Pair RSI signals with volume confirmation, moving average trends, or support/resistance levels. On Cluenex, pairing RSI with sentiment and options flow reduces false signals significantly.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the difference between a 14-period RSI and a 7-period RSI?
A 14-period RSI is slower and less responsive, producing fewer signals and fewer false positives. A 7-period RSI reacts faster to recent price action, producing more signals but also more false signals. Use 14 for swing trading (holding for days to weeks) and 7 or 9 for day trading or scalping.
Is RSI more effective on stocks or crypto?
RSI works on both, but crypto exhibits more extreme moves. Cryptoassets stay above RSI 70 and below RSI 30 longer than equities, so crypto traders often adjust thresholds to 80/20 instead of 70/30. Equities tend to respect the traditional 70/30 levels more consistently.
Can RSI predict a crash?
RSI cannot predict magnitude or timing of crashes, but extreme RSI readings (above 85 or below 15) combined with bearish divergence often precede sharp reversals. During market crashes, RSI may hit 5–10, but trying to bottom-pick with only RSI is dangerous. Always use stop losses.
Should I use RSI on a 1-minute chart?
Yes, but use a 5 or 7-period RSI instead of 14. Shorter timeframes require shorter lookback windows. A 14-period RSI on a 1-minute chart is too slow and lags price moves, making entries late.
What's the success rate of RSI overbought/oversold signals?
Overbought and oversold signals alone have a roughly 50% win rate—little better than a coin flip. But when combined with divergence, price action confirmation, or additional indicators (volume, sentiment, options flow), the win rate climbs to 65–75%.
Does RSI work on all stocks or only liquid ones?
RSI works best on liquid, heavily-traded stocks where buyers and sellers are plentiful (AAPL, MSFT, NVDA, TSLA). Illiquid or low-volume stocks can have choppy RSI that whipsaws frequently. Always check average daily volume before relying on RSI signals.
